Opus 355: The Commercial Town Hall – An Open-Source Blueprint for the Distributed Age
1. Introduction: The Architectural Imperative of the Commercial Town Hall
The contemporary workplace is currently undergoing a metamorphosis as profound as the shift from the cellular office to the open plan in the mid-20th century. The post-pandemic landscape has rendered the traditional "satellite office"—a static container for desks and monitors—obsolete. In its place, a new typology is emerging: the "Commercial Town Hall." This concept re-imagines the suburban office suite not as a site of mandatory attendance and individual production, but as a voluntary destination for civic engagement, cultural transmission, and high-fidelity collaboration.
The project at 355 Main Street in Armonk, New York—christened "Opus 355"—serves as the prototypical case study for this transformation. Situated within the Armonk Professional Center, a campus that bridges the mid-century modern aesthetic with contemporary glass-and-steel interventions, the site offers a unique architectural canvas.1 The strategy detailed in this report repurposes a 7,590-square-foot suite 2 into a single-user, open-source platform that integrates the human-centric design philosophy of Vitra’s "Club Office" with a resilient, invisible technological backbone.
This report functions as a comprehensive blueprint, dissecting the convergence of architecture, social topology, and infrastructure. It posits that the modern office must operate like software: open-source, hackable, and built on a robust stack of technologies—specifically Casambi wireless mesh, DanteAV audiovisual distribution, and Lightpath 100G fiber connectivity—that allow the physical space to evolve at the speed of the organization it houses.
1.1 The Context: From Municipal to Commercial Governance
Armonk, New York, possesses a rich history of civic gathering. The original North Castle Town Hall, dating back to 1949, was a symbol of fiscal responsibility and community centralization, famously built with capital reserve funds rather than debt.3 As the municipality explores new developments for its civic functions 4, the private sector has an opportunity to adopt the "Town Hall" nomenclature to describe a new kind of corporate asset.
The "Commercial Town Hall" at Opus 355 is designed to function as the governance center for a distributed workforce. Just as a municipal town hall is the locus of identity, debate, and decision-making for a citizenry, Opus 355 is the locus for the professional identity of a company's workforce. It is where the "citizens" (employees) gather not to process paperwork—which can be done remotely—but to build culture, align on strategy, and engage in the rituals of professional community.5 This strategic pivot transforms the real estate asset from a liability (rent for empty desks) into a high-performance asset (a tool for talent retention and cultural cohesion).
1.2 The Open-Source Strategy
The "open-source" methodology applied to Opus 355 refers to a design and operational philosophy that prioritizes modularity, interoperability, and transparency.
Architectural Openness: The "Whitebox" condition 2 removes rigid partitions, allowing the user to reconfigure the "source code" of the layout using flexible furniture systems like Vitra’s Dancing Walls.2
Technological Interoperability: By rejecting proprietary, closed-loop AV and lighting systems in favor of standard protocols like Bluetooth Low Energy (Casambi) and IP-based networking (DanteAV), the infrastructure avoids vendor lock-in. It allows the tenant to integrate new hardware (sensors, cameras, screens) seamlessly, much like adding libraries to a software codebase.6
Transparent Intent: The strategy is documented and exposed, creating a "knowledgeable and intent buyer" who understands that the value of the office lies not in the drywall, but in the intelligent systems and social affordances embedded within it.
2. Design & Architecture: Zoning the Commercial Town Hall
The architectural intervention at 355 Main Street is an exercise in "editing" rather than "overwriting." The goal is to strip back the accumulated layers of previous fit-outs to reveal the building's essential character, and then to overlay a new zoning logic that creates a gradient of privacy and interaction.
2.1 The Lantern: The Beacon of Transparency
The defining architectural feature of 355 Main Street is the lobby addition designed by architect Vatche Simonian.1 This structure acts as the "Lantern" of the Commercial Town Hall.
The Glass Enclosure: The two-story glass façade creates a visual permeability that is critical to the Town Hall concept.8 In urban design, a town hall must be visible and accessible; similarly, the glass enclosure allows the internal energy of the Opus 355 "Town Square" to be visible from the street, turning the office into a lantern that signals activity and life. This transparency breaks down the fortress-like quality of traditional suburban office parks.
Material Continuity: The elevator tower, clad in oversized stone panels, provides a vertical anchor. These panels continue from the exterior into the interior lobby, dissolving the threshold between the public realm (the street) and the semi-private realm (the office).9 This continuity reinforces the "street-to-suite" experience, suggesting that the office is an extension of the community rather than a separate, sealed entity.
Functional Aesthetics: The stone cladding performs a dual function: it provides a monumental aesthetic appropriate for a "Town Hall" while cleverly concealing the mechanical room doors.9 This creates a clean, uncluttered visual narrative that elevates the arrival experience.
2.2 Mapping the Design Development (DD) Plans to the Town Hall Concept
The Design Development (DD) plans 2 provide the chassis upon which the "Commercial Town Hall" is built. The renovation strategy involves a "demolition to whitebox" approach, removing oil-based primers, glues, and non-essential partitions to create a neutral, flexible canvas.2
The floor plan is then re-zoned into distinct functional territories that mirror the urban planning of a city: The Public Square, The Guild Halls (Semi-Public), and The Residences (Private).

2.2.1 The Public Square: Entry and Café
The entry sequence (Zone 2) is designed to be transitional rather than static. The DD plans indicate the removal of restrictive wall-mounted telephony and the smoothing of partitions to create a fluid ingress.2 This area flows directly into the "Town Square" (Zone 8) and the Café (Zone 10), ensuring that visitors are immediately immersed in the energy of the organization.
The Café (Break Room 213) is positioned as the hearth. The architectural plans specify the removal of abandoned appliances and the installation of a new sink location, utilizing existing water lines to minimize plumbing costs while maximizing utility.2 This area is treated not as a back-of-house kitchenette but as a front-of-house hospitality zone, featuring high-end finishes like "White Thunder" quartz 2 to signal quality and care.
2.2.2 The Green Nook: Biophilic Acupuncture
A critical component of the design is the "Green Nook" (Zone 3). While the DD plans show this as a transitional space near the conference rooms, the design intent elevates it into a destination. This area creates a "soft edge" in the architecture—a place of refuge. The architectural intervention here involves the use of Daltile "Conrad Brick" in a glossy finish 2, which introduces texture and reflects the warm light from the "Lantern" area. This nook serves as the psychological counterbalance to the high-activity zones, providing a space for decompression and informal, intimate conversation.
2.2.3 The Residences: Private Offices as Cockpits
The perimeter offices (Zones 1, 4, 5, 6) retain their physical enclosures to provide necessary acoustic isolation for high-focus work. However, the demolition notes call for the removal of legacy flooring and the installation of new Shaw Carpet Tile 2, creating a distinct acoustic environment from the hard surfaces of the public areas. These rooms are not hierarchical "corner offices" in the traditional sense but are programmed as "cockpits"—highly functional, tech-enabled spaces for execution. The executive suite (Zone 1/Office 201) is spacious enough (17'7" x 17'7") 2 to function as a private meeting room, effectively doubling the suite's collaborative capacity.
2.3 The Ceilingscape and Vertical Zoning
The Reflected Ceiling Plan (RCP) 2 plays a crucial role in defining these zones without physical walls.
The Grid: The decision to paint the existing grid black in open areas 2 creates a visual void that raises the perceived ceiling height and creates a "studio" aesthetic.
The Clouds: In the private offices and conference rooms, the use of Armstrong Dune Tegular tiles 2 reintroduces the ceiling plane to provide acoustic dampening and light reflectance, creating a more intimate, focused atmosphere.
The Luminaires: The integration of BASO linear fixtures and Coronet LSR1 recessed lights reinforces the architectural lines. The BASO fixtures, running parallel to the window lines, draw the eye outward, expanding the sense of space.10
3. Furniture & Social Spaces: The Vitra Club Office Implementation
If the architecture provides the skeleton of the Commercial Town Hall, the furniture provides the organs. The strategy employs the Vitra Club Office concept 12, a methodology that acknowledges the hybrid nature of contemporary work by dividing the office into three specific modes: Public, Semi-Public, and Private.
3.1 The Philosophy of the Club
The Club Office concept is rooted in the idea that employees return to the office not for the things they have at home (a desk and a laptop), but for the things they don't: mentorship, serendipitous encounters, and a sense of belonging.13 The furniture at Opus 355 is selected to act as micro-architecture, defining space and behavior through form and materiality.
3.2 Detailed Zone Configurations and Specifications
Zone 1: The Executive Hub (Office 201)
This zone redefines executive presence. Instead of a barrier desk, the room is anchored by the Joyn 2 Touchdown 2, a large communal table that invites team members to "pull up a chair."
Seating: Chair.04 by Maarten Van Severen offers a flexible polyurethane shell that supports active sitting, moving away from the bulky "throne" aesthetic of traditional executive chairs.2
Agility: The inclusion of the Dancing Wall 2—a mobile partition that functions as a whiteboard and acoustic barrier—allows the executive to instantly transform the room from a private workspace to a strategy war room. This fluidity is essential for modern leadership, which is often participatory rather than directive.
Zone 2: The Concierge (Reception)
First impressions in the Commercial Town Hall are about hospitality, not security.
The Prouvé Legacy: The area features the Fauteuil Direction armchair and Table Guéridon by Jean Prouvé.2 These pieces, designed in the mid-20th century, bring a structural honesty and warmth (oak and steel) that resonates with the building's 1960s heritage.
Cultural Display: The Rayonnage Mural shelving 2 acts as a gallery wall for the company's artifacts, awards, or library, immediately signaling the organization's culture to anyone entering.
Zone 3: The Green Velvet Nook (Lounge)
This is the emotional center of the office, designed for low-frequency, high-intimacy interaction.
The Alcove: The centerpiece is the Vitra Alcove Highback Sofa by Ronan & Erwan Bouroullec.2 The high side and back panels create a "room within a room," providing acoustic isolation without the need for drywall construction.14
The Green Velvet Strategy: While the proposal allows for various fabrics, the "Green Velvet" specification (likely referencing a custom application or a high-grade Laser/Credo fabric in a verdant hue) is strategic.15 Green evokes restoration and nature (biophilia), while velvet offers a tactile richness that triggers a relaxation response, contrasting with the sleek surfaces of personal devices. This nook becomes the designated "slow lane" of the office.
Lighting: The Louis Poulsen PH 5 pendant hangs low over the Belleville Table.2 Its glare-free design creates a warm pool of light that draws people in, mimicking the intimacy of a dining table.
Zone 8: The Dynamic Town Square (Open Office 222)
This 27' x 38' expanse 2 is the engine room of the Commercial Town Hall. It is designed to be aggressively flexible.
Configuration A (The Workshop): MedaMorph Folding Tables 2 are linked to create large workbenches for project teams.
Configuration B (The Symposium): Tables are folded and nested. Stool-Tools 2—monolithic, stackable seating units—are scattered to create an amphitheater arrangement for all-hands meetings. The Dancing Walls are rolled into position to serve as presentation surfaces or to divide the room into breakout clusters.12
Behavioral Design: The HAL RE Tube chairs 2 are made from recycled plastic, reinforcing the sustainability narrative. Their lightweight nature encourages users to move them, fostering a sense of ownership over the space.
Zone 9: The Watering Hole (Break Room 213)
Elevation: Bistro Stand-Up Tables 2 encourage standing posture, which is critical for quick, energetic exchanges.
The Nordic Touch: Artek Barstool 64 2 brings birch wood into the palette. The natural grain of the wood provides a subconscious connection to nature, softening the "break room" aesthetic and elevating it to a "kitchen island" feel.
Zone 10: The Diner (Café)
The Soft Work Revolution: The Soft Work sofa system 2 creates a landscape of seating that sits between a task chair and a lounge sofa. With integrated power sockets and pivot tables, it acknowledges that modern work often happens away from the desk.18 It transforms the corridor into a productive workspace, activating the "in-between" spaces of the floor plan.
4. 'Under the Hood' Technology: The Invisible Nervous System
The physical architecture of Opus 355 is animated by a digital infrastructure that is robust, scalable, and invisible. The technology stack is designed to support the "Open-Source" strategy: it is protocol-based rather than proprietary, ensuring future compatibility.
4.1 Lighting Control: The Casambi Wireless Mesh
Lighting in the Commercial Town Hall is not just utility; it is a spatial organizer. The system relies on Casambi, a Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) wireless mesh technology.6
4.1.1 The Mesh Architecture
Unlike traditional DALI systems that require complex control wiring (0-10V) running to every switch, Casambi creates a self-organizing mesh network. Every fixture acts as a node. If one node fails, the signal reroutes through neighbors, creating a self-healing system with no single point of failure.19
The Hardware Layer: The design utilizes 28 units of XNAPPK DALI-0-10V-1CH-CAS room controllers.2 These devices sit in the ceiling plenum, converting standard LED drivers into intelligent wireless nodes. This allows Opus 355 to use best-in-class architectural fixtures (like BASO and Coronet) while controlling them via the Casambi protocol.
The Sensory Layer: The space is gridded with 32 units of XNASSR-PDS-BAT-CAS sensors.2 These are battery-powered, ceiling-mounted sensors that detect both occupancy (PIR) and ambient light levels (LUX).
Granular Data: Because the sensors are wireless, they can be deployed densely without wiring costs. This provides high-resolution data on how the "Town Hall" is being used, allowing for heat-mapping of zones.
Energy Logic: In "Vacancy Mode" (Open Office), lights must be manually turned on but auto-off when empty. In "Occupancy Mode" (Private Offices), they are auto-on/auto-off. This optimizes energy usage without annoying users.21
4.1.2 Human-Centric Illumination
The system is programmed for "Tunable White" capabilities.
Circadian Rhythm: In the morning, the BASO linear fixtures 10 emit a cool 4000K-5000K light to suppress melatonin and boost alertness. As the day progresses towards the "Town Hall" evening events, the system shifts to a warm 3000K, creating a "sunset" effect that signals a transition from focus work to social connection.22
Scene Logic: A WS-TA08 8-button switch 2 in Zone 8 allows for instant scene recall. A "Presentation" scene dims the overhead BASO lights to 20% and engages the wall-washers to highlight the speaker, altering the room's focal point instantly.
4.2 Audiovisual Distribution: The DanteAV Ecosystem
To function as a Town Hall, the space must support broadcast-quality communication. The strategy bypasses clunky HDMI matrix switchers in favor of DanteAV (AV over IP).7
4.2.1 Unified Transport Protocol
DanteAV packetizes video and audio into IP packets that travel over standard Cat6 ethernet cables.23
Video Nodes: The "Town Hall" (Zone 8) is equipped with Bolin D412 PTZ Cameras.24 These native DanteAV cameras capture 4K60 video and transmit it with sub-millisecond latency. This ensures that when a speaker's image is projected on the screen, their lip movement is perfectly synchronized with the audio—critical for maintaining the illusion of presence in hybrid meetings.
Audio Integration: Ceiling microphones (Shure MXA series) capture questions from the floor during a Town Hall. Because both audio and video are on the Dante network, they are synchronized via Precision Time Protocol (PTP) 23, eliminating the "lip-flap" lag common in Zoom calls.
4.2.2 The "Overflow" Capability
Because DanteAV operates on the standard network, any screen in the office can become an overflow monitor. If the Town Square (Zone 8) is full, the feed can be routed instantly to the displays in the Café (Zone 10) or the Executive Office (Zone 1) via the Dante Controller software.25 This creates a "campus-wide" broadcast capability without running dedicated video cabling.
4.3 Connectivity: The Lightpath 100G Fiber Backbone
The nervous system of this operation is Lightpath’s Dedicated Fiber Internet Access (DIA).26
4.3.1 Bandwidth for the Future
100 Gbps Symmetrical: The facility connects via a 100 Gbps fiber link. This immense bandwidth is necessary to support the uncompressed video streams of DanteAV (which can consume ~800Mbps per 4K stream 27) alongside standard corporate data traffic.
Low Latency & Jitter: For real-time applications like DanteAV, "jitter" (the variation in packet arrival time) is the enemy. Lightpath’s all-fiber optical network offers superior jitter performance compared to copper-based broadband 28, ensuring that the AV sync remains rock-solid.
Scalability: The "open-source" nature of the connection allows bandwidth to be "bursted".29 If the tenant decides to host a media-heavy hackathon or live-stream a global event, the capacity is available on demand.
4.4 The Luminous Environment: Fixture Specification
BASO Linear Systems: Used in Zone 8 and private offices, BASO 2.5 and 4.0 recessed fixtures provide the primary ambient light.10 Their high-performance opal covers diffuse light to prevent screen glare. BASO Wall Washers are deployed on perimeter walls to vertically illuminate surfaces, making the suites feel more spacious.
Coronet LSR1: In areas with shallow plenums (HVAC bulkheads), the Coronet LSR1 Low Profile fixture 11 is used. Its engineering allows it to fit in extremely tight ceiling spaces while maintaining the same linear aesthetic as the BASO fixtures, ensuring visual continuity.
Louis Poulsen PH 5: Suspended in the "Green Nook" and over collaborative tables, the PH 5 utilizes a three-shade system to direct light downward and laterally without any direct glare from the bulb.17 This creates an island of "hygge" (coziness) within the technical workspace.
5. Finishes & Millwork: The Tactile Interface
The finish strategy balances the economic pragmatism of an "open-source" model with the tactile luxury required for a "Club" atmosphere.
5.1 The "IKEA Hack" Millwork Strategy
To maximize budget for technology and furniture, the millwork strategy utilizes high-quality IKEA carcass modules as a base, clad in premium custom skins.2
The Tuxedo Aesthetic: In the Break Room (Zone 9), base cabinets are clad in Wilsonart Traceless "Black Velvet" (15505) laminate.2 This nanotechnology surface prevents fingerprints, maintaining a pristine matte look despite high traffic.
Natural Contrast: Upper cabinetry uses Wilsonart "Beigewood" (7850-60) 2, introducing a vertical grain that warms the space and connects visually with the Artek birch stools.
5.2 Countertops: The Stone Surfaces
Hygiene and Aesthetics: The Break Room and Café feature Caesarstone "Pure White" (1141) quartz.2 This non-porous surface is impervious to coffee stains and bacteria, essential for a communal kitchen. Its reflective quality helps bounce light deep into the room.
Durability in Production: The Copy/Resource area (Zone 7) utilizes Midnight Corvo quartz.2 This darker, complex stone hides toner dust and ink marks, ensuring the utility area looks maintained.
5.3 Wall Treatments and Flooring
Texture in the Nook: The "Green Nook" is defined not just by furniture but by Daltile "Conrad Brick" tiles in Siren Glossy.2 The uneven, hand-molded texture of the brick contrasts with the smooth drywall and glass, adding a layer of historical resonance that aligns with the "Town Hall" concept.
Zoned Flooring: The open areas utilize Mohawk LVT "Hot & Heavy" (Concrete look).2 This provides the acoustic reflectivity needed for a lively "Town Square" atmosphere and allows furniture on casters (like Dancing Walls) to roll smoothly. Private offices switch to Shaw Carpet Tile 2, signaling a transition to the "quiet zone."
6. Conclusion: The Living Blueprint
Opus 355 represents a radical departure from the static office leasing model. By integrating the civic gravity of a "Commercial Town Hall" with the domestic comfort of the Vitra "Club Office," and powering it with a nervous system of Casambi, DanteAV, and Lightpath fiber, the space becomes a living organism.
This open-source strategy empowers the tenant to "hack" their environment—reconfiguring walls, lights, and data flows to meet the evolving needs of their business. It is a workspace designed not just for efficiency, but for resilience, connection, and the celebration of professional community. In the distributed age, 355 Main Street stands as a beacon—a lantern on the hill—demonstrating that the office is not dead; it has simply evolved into something far more vital.
